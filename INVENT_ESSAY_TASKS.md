1. Assess the sharpness of the cat as a complete subject within the photograph. Look at the cat in isolation — its fur edges, the contours of its ears and eyes, its whiskers, the markings in its coat — and determine how crisply and clearly the entire cat has been rendered. Consider the cat as a whole rather than focusing only on its most prominent feature; a cat whose eyes are sharp but whose body is soft is not the same as a cat that is uniformly crisp throughout. The result of this assessment is a judgment of the cat's visual presence: whether it has been given the gift of sharpness or whether it is dissolving and fading from the viewer's ability to perceive it in detail.

2. Assess the softness and visual recession of everything in the photograph that is not the cat. Examine the background, the foreground, and all surrounding elements — furniture, foliage, textures, patterns, other objects — and determine the degree to which they have become blurred, indistinct, and subordinate. Recognize that a rich, detailed environment which has dissolved into smooth softness is actively doing work to redirect the viewer's eye away from itself and back toward the subject. Account for the fact that sparse or featureless surroundings offer less opportunity for visible recession than busy, complex environments. The result of this assessment is a judgment of how much the environment has quieted itself and deferred to the cat.

3. Assess the magnitude of the gap between the cat's sharpness and the softness of its surroundings, and produce the final score. This is not simply a confirmation that the cat is sharper than the background — it is an evaluation of how wide the difference is. A narrow gap, where the cat is only marginally crisper than the world around it, produces a weak sense of focus distinction and a lower score. A wide gap, where the cat is the sole island of clarity in a sea of softness, produces an unmistakable sense of visual priority and a higher score. When the cat is softer than its surroundings — when the environment is sharper than the subject — the score should reflect that the focus distinction has been inverted and the cat has been denied visual priority. Synthesize the findings from the first two tasks into a single scalar score between 0.0 and 1.0 that expresses how powerfully the cat emerges from its environment as the undeniable point of focus.
